Interview

General Advice

Interview questions do not have a right answer.

The goal is not to be as correct as possible.

There is no checklist of "things the student should have mentioned".

An interview is a conversation, and the objective of the professors is to gain an understanding of how you think, and how you communicate those thoughts with others. The ability to explain a concept well to another person is a great marker of your own understanding, and communication with others is an essential part of any field.

I've brought in some subject experts to give more context and advice for interviews across a variety of subjects. This should cover a reasonable enough range so you can infer how your subject interview will roughly look like, even if it's not actually on the list.

Enjoy, and one more thing - you may get asked questions about your personal statement. These are the easiest questions to prepare for and ace, so don't get caught slacking!

Hello! I’m an anonymous contributor who studied Law at the University of Cambridge from 2017 to 2020. I work at a Magic Circle law firm in London as an Associate (and therefore cannot do a video like the other contributors on this page), and worked (throughout university and during my training contract) as a freelance Oxbridge law admissions tutor for private tutoring companies in my free time for around 4 years.

Questions asked in an Oxbridge law interview can typically be split into two types - general interest / personal statement questions and logical reasoning exercises.

General Interest Questions

For general interest questions, these are usually ice-breaker questions aimed at making students more comfortable in the beginning of an interview. Not all interviews will include these and there are students who are not asked any general interest / personal statement interview questions. However, this is still an important section to prepare for because failing to eloquently explain why you want to study law won’t help your chances and will also make you more nervous for the remainder of the interview. These types of questions revolve around your interest in law, the Cambridge/Oxford law course (as applicable) and your personal statement.

Read on

In your preparation, I would recommend going through your personal statement sentence by sentence and thinking about what kinds of questions you could be asked about the various topics you have discussed (you could even throw your personal statement into ChatGPT and ask it to come up with questions for you). If you have mentioned a book / article / case, make sure to re-read it before your interview as you should be prepared to discuss it in detail if asked.

Logical Reasoning Exercises - Advice

As an admissions tutor, the goal with my students was to help them understand how to:

  1. calmly consider and logically think through the questions at hand;
  2. articulate their thoughts on the point clearly and succinctly; and
  3. be able to strike the right balance between (a) considering new information and perspectives and shifting their position and (b) backing themselves and standing by what they have previously said.

The Oxbridge law interviews are unique compared to that of some other subjects (eg mathematics, natural sciences) in that zero prior knowledge is expected. Most students do not study law at A levels and therefore the interview is designed to be an even playing ground aimed at focusing on your logical reasoning abilities and your intellectual flexibility. On that basis, the following are the key pieces of advice I have for logical reasoning interviews:

  1. If you are asked to apply legislation to a fact pattern, stick closely to the legislation at hand. If we take Exercise 1 from the first sample interview link above, you should remember that the key points of the definition of murder here are “intentionally” and “cause”. Always answer the fact patterns on the basis of the legislation provided in the first instance. If you have any external knowledge (eg cases you have read, defences to murder that you know about), these should only come in after that and only if actually relevant to your logical reasoning (ie don’t talk about them just to show that you have done further reading). Bringing in external content, especially if you are just being asked to apply legislation to facts, will detract from the exercise and make it harder for the interviewers to see your logical thinking process.
  2. Do not see the interviewer as the enemy: a common mistake students make is refusing to budge from their positions once they have made them, even when the interviewer suggests a different perspective. The students that do this forget that this exercise is intended to be a conversation that shows how you think and that there is probably no single right answer to any of these questions. The interviewer isn’t trying to trap you and you maintaining a position regardless of how insane the factual patterns become will not impress anyone. Be comfortable with reassessing your logical reasoning - consider whether or not you agree with the alternative position being set forth by the interviewer and/or if it could raise any additional issues.
  3. Believe in your own logical reasoning ability: the flip side of point 2 above is that you equally should not flip flop every time the interviewer casts doubt on your position (which will be frequently). When considering additional information and/or perspectives, you should be able to see if they affect the logical basis of your argument. If you don’t think that it does, explain why.

Some of the logical reasoning interviews will come with pre-reading and you’ll be given time ahead of your interview to read and consider the text. This can range from 10 to 30 minutes depending on the length of the text. I won’t give advice on how to approach this because all students process information differently (eg some of you will prefer to skim the whole thing once and then revisit sections in detail, whilst others may prefer to read it in detail once through), but I will say that your goal should be able to know the following by the end of your allocated time:

  1. For a legal judgment: what were the facts of the case and what was the verdict? If there were multiple judges giving judgments, which ones were for the verdict and which ones dissented and why? How did the reasoning of the judges differ between one another? Do you agree with the verdict and/or any of the dissenting judges?
  2. For a piece of legislation: what are the components needed for a set of facts to fall within the scope of the legislation (eg for a crime, what would someone need to do in order for their actions to constitute this crime)? Are there exceptions and/or defences in relation to this legislation (and, if not, do you think there should be any)?

Logical Reasoning Exercises - An Example

Let’s walk through part of a logical reasoning exercise together, taking the same Exercise 1 from the first sample interview link. I’ve set out the sample questions below, and have included a few thoughts in italics. As noted above, there is no single right answer to the questions below and the points set out below are merely my own interpretation/view on the questions.

First exercise. Consider the following definition of murder: ‘a person is guilty of murder if he intentionally causes the death of another person.’

As above, the key point you should get here is what the components of murder are, being intentionality and causation.

What do you think ‘intentionally’ means in this context?

There really is no right answer for this - think about how you would describe a person’s intention when trying to murder someone. What would they be thinking about, what do you think they should know, what state of mind would this person be in?

As an example, I could define “intentionally” as a person doing an action with the knowledge that that action could reasonably likely result in a specific consequence.

So am I guilty if I kill a pilot of an aeroplane I own by blowing up the aeroplane over the ocean in order to claim the insurance?

This is the first fact pattern. Apply the legislation here - what can you link here to intentionality (in particular your definition of it above) and causation, with the focus being on intentionality since you have just defined it? Does this fact pattern make you want to alter your definition of intentionality, whether that is to add something to it or to make something more/less specific?

With my definition, yes I am guilty because (1) intention is satisfied as I knew that blowing up the plane would be reasonably likely to kill the pilot and (2) causation is satisfied because I blew up the plane.

What if I poison a person’s tea, hoping to kill him, but with a poison that only works 0.005% of the time: do I intend his death?

Second fact pattern. The point here is to introduce the variable of probability. Do you think it affects my intention if there is only a 0.005% chance of death? Is a 0.005% chance enough? If not, what threshold would you think is sufficient? If it is enough, is any chance of death sufficient or if there a threshold?

Using my definition, this might fail to satisfy the definition of murder because I had a concept of “reasonably likely” that 0.005% is unlikely to satisfy. I would have to consider if I think “reasonably likely” is appropriate to include in my definition of intentionality and what threshold “reasonably likely” would be and how this could be applied in practice. I may decide to alter my definition to fit with what I think the outcome should be here (eg change the definition to a person doing an action with the knowledge that it could result in a specific consequence, regardless of how remote that possibility is).

What if I separate conjoined twins knowing that if I don’t both will soon die, but if I do one will die now but one might survive to live for many years?

Third fact pattern - this introduces an alternative motive. How is intentionality affected if you could argue that my main intention was to save someone’s life, even if that then resulted in another person’s death?

For most definitions of intentionality, this will satisfy murder because (1) I separate the conjoined twins and (2) I know that this could / is reasonably likely to result in the death of one of the twins.

This might make you uncomfortable because you may feel that convicting me of murder in this scenario is not appropriate and/or just. However, with the definition of murder given, it’s difficult to see how this would ever not constitute murder. It would be difficult to come up with reasonable definitions for causation and intentionality which wouldn’t deem this to be murder. So then you should start thinking about alternatives - what defences or exceptions do you think should be included in the legislation?

Do you think I should be convicted of murder in this scenario? Why or why not? What would the defence be and how would it be regulated? What criteria would you need to meet and do you need any expert input (eg the input of medical professionals on the viability of the lives of the conjoined twins)?

The other difficult concept in the definition is ‘cause’: how would you describe what ‘cause’ means?

As above, explain what you understand causation to mean in the context of murder. I could define it to mean that I do an action that directly results in someone’s death.

What if I am giving someone a lift in my car and I say ‘It is a lovely day’ and they irrationally leap out of the car in fright thinking I am going to kill them, and injure themselves so badly they die: have I caused the injury?

Apply your definition. Note here that they have asked if you have caused the injury and are not asking whether or not this constitutes murder. This challenges you to focus your thoughts on the question at hand. It’s a common reaction to want to say that this doesn’t constitute causation because it feels uncomfortable to think of this as me causing the death when the other person’s reaction was so unpredictable, but if this fits your definition of causation then I have caused the death. Remember that murder has two elements for a reason - even if causation is satisfied, intentionality may not be satisfied.

With my definition above, yes my action does constitute causation. My words prompted a reaction (however unreasonable / unpredictable), which resulted in their life-threatening injuries that ultimately killed them.

What if the victim could be saved but refuses a blood transfusion because they are biased against Australian doctors and so they die?

This is intended to introduce the concept of a chain of causation. If something happens in between my action and their death, does that break the chain of causation from my action and their death? What criteria should something meet in order to break that chain of causation?

Using my definition, I can argue that they didn’t cause this because their death is no longer a direct result of my actions. They made an active choice to refuse a blood transfusion because of their biases.

But I can equally argue that I still caused their death because their biases are part of who they are as a person, and culpability shouldn’t be removed just because they have these biases. This might not seem to make sense with the fact pattern above, but consider if the facts were altered a bit - what if instead of being racist against Australian doctors, I’m refusing a blood transfusion because of my religious beliefs? How does that affect your view on these facts?

As you can see from the above, even with these few questions, there are so many possibilities in your responses and there is no right answer! Focus on making logical arguments with the information you have been given.
