Preparation Strategy

I'm going to limit this discussion to the maths/physics-y admissions assessments (ESAT/PAT/TMUA/MAT/STEP) here. Before we get into the details of each, we'll talk generally preparation strategy.

--> Hop to any of | ESAT | PAT | TMUA | MAT | STEP |

Having tutored admissions tests for hundreds of hours, I can safely say that they are absolutely no different whatsoever from any other kind of exam. Meaning:

  • The tests have their own quirks that take getting used to.
  • The best way of preparing is to do every past paper that's ever existed.

For most of these, PMT/Official university websites have worked answers. I would recommended giving yourself a day or so between doing a question, and looking at its answers. Let your subconcious make progress on the problem - you'll be amazed at what you can discover in your sleep - but do look eventually! It's a continuous process of monitoring your progress and learning from your mistakes.

Generally speaking, STEM admissions tests give candidates loads of medium difficulty questions within a very tight time frame. This varies by exam - I would put ESAT on the easier/faster end of the spectrum, and STEP at the harder/slower end. In all cases, time will be an extreme limiter. Unlike A levels, you will likely neither finish the exam nor score 100%. This is intended as a way of separating the best from the very best! Also, they're based on content that you should have covered in school by this point. Do check this!!

If you can find questions filtered by topic, that can be a nice way to approach learning the exam. In doing 20 TMUA ""if/iff"" questions, you'll learn the pattern and strategy faster than by seeing one every now and again.

Also, broadly speaking, all of these papers are useful to revise for the others - so there's no risk of running out of past papers.

ESAT

Website, Specification, and Past Papers.

If you like Maths and are applying for NatSci, I might encourage you to take both Maths papers. A strong mathematics performance is looked on relatively favourably, and it could reduce the diversity of content you need to prepare for. Have a look at past papers - this is a new exam (it merged the NSAA for NatSci and ENGAA for engineering), so you can use the historical bank of these as resources to practice. It's pretty standard in its questioning, and a solid understanding of most year 1 content e.g. algebra, geometry, trigonometry, calculus should get you there.

This exam avoids "advanced" content (e.g. integrating trig functions) and will instead ask harder questions on "basic" content, e.g. \( \int_{1}^{4} \frac{2x^2 - 3}{x\sqrt{x}} \, dx \), which is really just integrating \( x^n \) (separate out and simplify).

Extreme time crunch will become apparent once you try complete a paper in 60 or so minutes... you probably won't finish this exam in time, even if a lot of the questions are familiar and relatively zippy. Focus more on your percentage - try to hit 80% for a good shot at an interview (daunting at first, but it does get easier with practice!).



PAT

Website, Syllabus, and Past Papers. I'm afraid Oxford's less generous with their description of what's in the test.

The PAT has more maths-y physics questions than the relatively segregated ESAT. This makes it harder. It also requires knowledge of the solar system (eclipses, orbits, gravitational fields) and a higher chance to have random university level physics content (inertia, quantum physics). The former you should learn; the latter will come with all the information you need in the question.

It's critical not to panic when you see such a question - it will be unfamiliar to almost everyone. Do your best!

An example of this mixed content is an analysis of the half life equation \( N_x(t) = N_x(0)e^{-\lambda_x t} \), which requires integrating/differentiating exponentials as well as a good physical intuition for radioactive decay.

This exam is more for the slower working physicists who are unafraid of tougher problems, than the zippy ones (send this lot to the ESAT). Ultimately, however, it's the same situation - try to hit 80% for a good shot at an interview.



TMUA

Website, Syllabus, and Past Papers.

The TMUA has a lot of thought-provoking, mathematically phrased proof and reasoning questions. These will take a lot of getting used to (read: headaches), so dedicated a lot of time not just to upskilling on the maths front, but getting used to the questions:

For which one of the following statements can the fact that \( 122^2 + 162^2 = 202^2 \) be used to produce a counterexample?

If you're able to adjust to these, the TMUA only has 20 questions per paper, so definitely is a paper you should aim to finish, or at least take an educated guess on every question!



MAT

Website, Syllabus, and Past Papers.

The MAT begins with hard multiple choice questions, and finishes with hard open ended questions.

The MCQs are on year 12 maths, and will all have available a fast, elegant solution. If you sense yourself going down a rabbit hole of algebra with no end in sight, pause. Think. Try to find a better path:

For all real n, it is the case that \( n^4 + 1 = (n^2 + sqrt{2n} + 1)(n^2 − sqrt{2n} + 1) \). For which whole numbers \(n\) is \(n^4 + 4\) prime?

The two open ended questions at the end are some weird context/piece of maths that you're expected to analyse on the spot. How quickly you can adapt to this new context, and how well you can communicate this to an examiner, will determine your success here.

Maybe off kilter here, but the MAT is my favourite admissions assessment. The long questions are often beautiful and profound, and each part fits together and builds on the previous bits in an immensely satisfying way. If you derived a result in part ii), you'll probably need it for part iii).



STEP

Website, Syllabus, and Past Papers.

Lord have mercy on you.

Step is fiendish and even the most successful candidates find it taxing. The good news is, there is more targeted support for STEP than for the other admission tests.

The official support programme is one, this book of worked answers another, and this website of questions filtered by topic a third.

STEP looks like something like this:

Explain why \( \sum_{r=n}^{\infty} \frac{1}{r^2} \int_{n}^{\infty} \frac{dx}{x^2} \sum_{r=n+1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{r^2} \)
