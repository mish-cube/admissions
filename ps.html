A Personal Statement Is Best Done Incrementally Over Many Months

There's an absolutely outrageous amount of information you can view online about how to write a personal statement. You will notice very quickly that not all of it agrees - this is simply because there are many possible ways to write a personal statement, and everybody has their preference.

Your teachers, school counsellor, parents, tutors, friends etc. will likely all have different feedback for you when they read your personal statement. Understandably this can feel rather overwhelming!!

If it helps, for most STEM related subjects, the personal statement doesn't carry a huge amount of weight. It's more of a failure criterion than a success criterion (meaning that, as long as it's OK, it won't really affect your admissions prospects). As long as you show reasonable evidence for a) passion b) motivation and c) competence in your chosen subject, it doesn't need to be Shakespeare.

I've put a few sources here that I think answer important questions about the personal statement, but ultimately it's in your hands. Think of the things you've done that demonstrate the above three qualities, especially things that separate you from the average candidate. Write about them expressively but don't be cliché. If you're out of ideas and need more, go do something cool!

Impressive Supercurriculars

To clarify:

Extracurriculars are non-academic activities unrelated to your subject, beyond what your school required of you e.g. I did improv comedy in high school, and played in an orchestra.

Supercurriculars are academic activities related to your subject, beyond what your school required of you e.g. I sat in on a bunch of genetics lectures at my local university then helped a professor of onion science study why a particular viral infection was wiping out a bunch of commercially grown onions in the region.

The biggest recommendation I have here, is if you don't have some supercurricular that looks impressive, find one now and start working on it. If you're applying to Physics, for example, you might locate some open source telescope data and perform an analysis of exoplanets by following a relevant paper . This kind of thing is clearly evidence of passion (you've picked a cool area of science), motivation (nobody forced you to do this) and competence (reading/replicating the work of scientific papers is hard work). It's a great project to talk about in a personal statement. Textbooks you've read, lectures you've attended, internships you've completed and competitions you've crushed are all excellent too, but there's nothing like a good old here's something I did entirely of my own volition and capabilities that I think is pretty neat.

You certainly don't need something like this, but if you're reaallly struggling to figure out what to talk about, then perhaps the best option is to do something worth talking about!



Use of AI

Here's the official UCAS stance.

General guidance from ChatGPT (is my bungee jumping EPQ something I should mention here, if I talk about it in this way?), as well as reading over your PS and offering small bits of feedback, can be very useful.

Two don'ts:

  • DON'T have any sentences that "sound" AI generated. People sniff these things out and it gives the impression you haven't done a lot of editing by hand. You're risking the label of "Lazy".
  • DON'T ask ChatGPT/other to write your whole PS for you. I know this process is painful. But a PS generated by ChatGPT will have an almost identical format and style to every other AI generated PS - even if you perform a bunch of tweaks and edits to the individual sentences, the overall "feel" will still be there. You will put the professors to sleep.


UCAS References

Not personal statement, but the UCAS references section. Someone at your school (or if you're not at a school, someone that knows you academically or professionally) will write this.

For international students:

Your teacher/other may not be familiar with this process. The reference should largely function as a character summary:

XXXX easily stands out as the top of her cohort of 35, thanks to her relentless questioning, analytical problem solving skills and profound subject insights.

For all students:

If there are extenuating circumstances that may have affected your ability to perform (specifically thinking GCSEs/predicted A level grades here), STATE THEM HERE!! (get whoever's writing it to state them here!!)

At our school, students are only supported in taking 5 A levels. The fact that, of XXXX's 10 A levels, half are A* and half are A/B, is because they opted to self-teach these subjects with minimal school support. We were extremely impressed by XXXX's ambition and resolve in this undertaking.
