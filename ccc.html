College and Course Choices

Let's briefly interpret the below data, from 2025 Cambridge admissions.

Firstly, the outliers. Trinity has a well earned remarkable reputation for Maths (which leaks into Physics), massively skewing applications. The least popular colleges - Hughes Hall, St Edmund's, and Wolfson - are Postgraduate / Mature Student colleges. We can ignore these. Generally speaking (as an overall trend between colleges for a given subject) there's no crazy differences between the colleges, just slight variations in application rate and offer rate.

Secondly, some courses are definitely more competitive than others (e.g. shown here: Maths has a 1/8 admission rate compared to 1/5 for Natural Sciences) and it's worth being aware of this when you apply.

Regarding college choice... go and visit them if you get the chance! Size, proximity to city centre / proximity to lecture theatres and general reputation can all come into play. I went to a tiny college (Corpus Christi). It was cosy and central. Supermarkets were close by but the noise from tourists on the street was immense. In third year I had to bike 15 minutes for my Physics lectures and labs. It was effectively a random choice and I ended up absolutely loving it.

My advice would be to choose a subject that you're passionate about, and a college that feels like a good fit for you personally. Likely as not there will be some small statistical discrepancy, but genuine desire for a subject and a college are extremely noticeable, impactful and impossible to fake in an interview context. For admissions success and your own quality of life, follow this desire!




Other Admissions Information

While we're here, we may as well peek at some other statistics for some clarity regarding where exactly we stand on some demographic features. I feel I should add - please don’t let data like this put you off applying. It is an improving picture that Oxbridge is actively trying to address!

State vs Independent Schools

Around two thirds of UK students at Oxbridge come from state schools - a majority, but relatively lower than the distribution of high achieving students would suggest.

This is not just student preference - averaging across Oxford's 2020-2024 data, the admission rate for state students was 17.6%, and the admission rate for independent school students was 21.0%.

What this tells us is that there's definitely still a skew towards independent education in university admission BEYOND grades or student preference. We could speculate on this until the cows come home - undoubtedly there are a variety of access barriers more likely to impact someone who's applying from a state school. Teachers at independent schools are also more likely to have come from an Oxbridge background themselves, and therefore have relevant wisdom to pass on to their students.

Let's look at the ways some independent schools get ahead, and how a student outside of the system could replicate these:

  1. Putting you in touch with current students ("oh, Sam got into Oxford for that course last year. I'm sure they'll have some good advice for you!"):
    • You can try Reddit or The Student Room. Talking to people at Oxbridge events (see below) is great too!
  2. Running mock interviews:
    • The onus is on you here to proactively get people to interview you. If you ask your teachers they might help - provide them with questions (if needed) and prompt them beforehand to give leading hints when you're struggling. Peers within your subject can also help. You want people with subject knowledge for this, otherwise it becomes less of a conversation and more of an audiobook - which defeats the purpose an "interview".
  3. Going through admissions tests:
    • Easily done! Past papers are all you need, and some intrinsic motivation. Start as early as possible...
  4. Giving personal statement advice:
    • Ask adults around you to read it and give feedback, and perhaps a light sprinkling of support from LLMs ("does this part sound cliché?" / "is this competition worth mentioning?" / "on the whole, how does my tone come across?").
  5. Writing well styled references:
    • See Personal Statement
  6. Pointing you towards Oxbridge hosted events:
    • Find these online - like this!


Local vs International Students

Averaging out the admission rates from 2020-2024 we get the following:

UK Student Admission Rate: 18.5%

EU Student Admission Rate: 7.4%

Non-EU Student Admission Rate: 7.2%

Like for independent/state schools, I won't claim to have all the answers as to why this is the case or tell you that the admission process is an entirely fair system... however I can tell you for sure that the lack of familiarity international schools have with the UK system does play a part in this.

Two things I'll say for international candidates:

  • Your English needs to be excellent. An interview is designed to be a fluent and eloquent discussion of subject ideas. This is 10x harder when you're struggling for key terminology, or misinterpret what a professor has asked you.
  • You will need to communicate with your teachers how to write a UK style reference. Be polite, but bring them the information that they need. More on this in Personal Statement.
